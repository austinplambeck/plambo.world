Reimagining Mega-Cities

December 6th, 2021

Mega-cities. These sterile, human-made amalgamations of concrete and steel are both awe-inspiring and, from an environmental perspective, flawed in design. They're built to trap in heat. Congested with automobiles, power plants, and factories that lead to smog and poor air quality. They completely overtake the natural environment, with no trace of green on many of the streets. And yet even still, mega-cities are truly amazing creations that show what a collective of people is capable of.

But imagine if they were better, across every level.

After reading about the poor air quality in Los Angeles in recent weeks and stumbling upon this brilliant post from Atmos Magazine, I thought this would be a good first environmental topic to cover.

Read through these slides to get a more detailed, factual look at how to holistically cool a mega-city.

View this post on Instagram

A post shared by Atmos (@atmos)

I think the solutions laid out in Atmos' post are everything we need. So I'm going to try to give you a quick look at what it would be like if they were implemented—at least how it would go in my head.

Let's imagine you wake up in New Los Angeles. Like you're actually in Los Angeles. Not in Burbank or Inglewood or Santa Monica or Culver City or West Hollywood or— You get the point. Unlike the old one, New Los Angeles is dense. There aren't a million sub-cities connected by fifty different freeways that take an hour to drive 3 miles. So, you wake up, and you head out of your apartment with no car keys in hand. You pass verdant green gardens on rooftops and balconies and others that hang vertically along walls. When you get to the street, the smell of soil and greenery hits you—not car exhaust and garbage. The sidewalks are bustling with people, and there are few cars on the road. Instead, there are abundant safe bike lanes and efficient methods of public transportation—buses, taxis, subways, trains—powered by clean energy sources. And because you live in the New L.A., your commute to work is quickly accessible by these public services. You walk to the station alongside streets of light-gray, not ridiculously hot asphalt. You pass a wide array of species of trees and shrubs and flowers that are all growing wildly, providing both shade and carbon sequestration. You breathe in fresh air... and look up in awe at the gardens twisting around buildings like vines would a fence. With one quiet electric-powered train ride, you’ve made it to your destination.

Okay, I know this might seem like some solarpunk fantasy world, but just imagine if something like that existed! What if our collective group produced a biophilic city like that? Less pollution, less traffic, less heat, more greenspace interspersed within the humanspace.

And we actually are! Not in the United States, of course, but in the eco-city of Tengah, Singapore. A city currently in construction that puts pedestrians and bicyclists first, implements biophilic design everywhere, and will have community farming within one of its five residential districts.
